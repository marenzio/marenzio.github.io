Daniele V. Filippi

1. Introduction

On January 1st, 1594 Luca Marenzio signed the dedication of his Sesto libro de madrigali a cinque voci to Cinzio Aldobrandini Passeri (1551–1610), nephew of Pope Clement VIII and recently appointed cardinal. Aldobrandini was a distinguished patron of artists and poets, famously of Torquato Tasso; Battista Guarini, Giovanni Bardi and Angelo Grillo, among others, were part of his patronage network.1 The dedication, entirely constructed from genre-specific topoi and clichés, does not shed any further light on the circumstances of the composition and of the arrangement for publication. As is well known, after the death of his former patron Cardinal Luigi d’Este in 1586, Marenzio had served Ferdinando de’ Medici in Rome and in Florence (1587–1589 or 1590), then, back in Rome, had maintained multiple patronage connections, notably with Virginio Orsini (from 1589/90) and with Aldobrandini (probably from 1593).2 Meanwhile, his publication rhythm had considerably slowed down, compared to the years 1580 to 1587: the period 1588–1593 had seen only the publication of the Book for 4, 5, and 6 voices (1588) and of Book V a 6 (1591).

As if marking the end of this transitional phase or, rather, the beginning of a new chapter in Marenzio’s career, Book VI a 5 is characterized by a distinct change in the choice of texts, by a subtle montage, and by a noticeable stylistic shift.

Of the seventeen texts set in the book, four (plus one adaptation, see below) are by Battista Guarini, three by G.B. Strozzi the Elder, one each by Ridolfo Arlotti (c. 1550–1613), Livio Celiano, Antonio Ongaro (c. 1560–1592/1599),3 and Torquato Tasso; one text each is furthermore attributed to Bonifazio Bevilacqua and Annibale Caro, while three texts remain anonymous (see Table 1).4 Three aspects are remarkable: the absence of Petrarca and Sannazaro (who together accounted for fifteen texts out of twenty-one in Book I a 4 of 1585 and for eleven texts out of fifteen in the Book for 4, 5, and 6 voices of 1588);5 the presence of Strozzi, which, in spite of previous settings by Marenzio,6 is probably to refer to the appearance, in 1593, of a posthumous edition of his Madrigali;7 and the first occurrences of Livio Celiano (Angelo Grillo) and Antonio Ongaro, whose poems Marenzio will set again in the last books a 5 of the late 1590s.

The most innovative feature regards, however, Guarini: not so much for the presence and number of his texts (Marenzio had repeatedly set Guarini’s poetry since his first madrigal book, Book I a 5 of 1580), as for their provenance from a work of crucial importance in late sixteenth-century culture, Il pastor fido. While de Monte and Monteverdi published Pastor fido settings before Marenzio,8 the latter was, in the words of Marco Bizzarini, “the first of the leading madrigalists to set passages from Guarini’s drama in more than a sporadic way”.9 The present book and the following one in the five-voice series (VII a 5, 1595) “have for Il pastor fido a pioneering importance”.10 While the possibility of a direct contact between Marenzio and Guarini remains an undocumented speculation, and the circles of Aldobrandini and Cardinal Montalto might have played an instrumental role,11 it has recently been suggested that Cardinal Scipione Gonzaga (1542–1593) could have brought Il pastor fido to Marenzio’s attention “as a promising source of madrigal texts”.12 Be it as it may, Marenzio’s settings probably accompanied and fuelled the discussion about Guarini’s controversial play in Cardinal Aldobrandini’s ridotto (and elsewhere, after the publication), and offered a sort of alternative realization of some passages, as if on the virtual, polyphonic stage of the madrigal.13

As many scholars have noticed,14 some of the names occurring in the texts set by Marenzio have been systematically altered. In no. 2, Clori nel mio partire, no. 10, Or chi, Clori beata, no. 12, Clori mia, Clori dolce, oh sempre nuovo, and no. 15, “Rimanti in pace”, a la dolente e bella, the original “Flora”, “Filli”, or “Fillida” have been substituted by “Clori”/“Cloride”, while the first line in the Pastor fido passage set as no. 11, Deh, Tirsi, Tirsi, anima mia, perdona, originally sounded “E tu, Mirtillo, anima mia, perdona”.15 The two names also occur in nos. 6, Stillò l’anima in pianto (Tirsi and Clori), 16, Ecco Maggio seren, chi l’ha vestito (Clori, this time together with Filli), and 17, Cantiam la bella Clori (Clori only). Thus, Giuseppe Gerbino speaks of a “subtle but unmistakable narrative thread” that underlies the book: “the story of Tirsi and Clori”.16

Noticing that Tirsi was the pseudonym used by Virginio Orsini in his capacity as “prince” of the pastoral society known as “pastori della valle tiberina” (shepherds of the Tiber valley), James Chater has hypothesized that Tirsi and Clori should be identified as Virginio and his wife, Flavia Peretti.17 In this perspective, the final piece (no. 17, Cantiam la bella Clori), whose epithalamic character has often been pointed out, should be interpreted as a nuptial homage to Flavia, whom Virginio had married in 1589. But besides the fact that Marenzio had already included a tribute to the pair in Book V a 6 in 1591,18 a new piece of information further complicates the matter.

In 1595, just one year after the publication of our book, Marenzio dedicated his Book VI a 6 to Margherita Gonzaga (1564–1618); the opening piece, Lucida perla a cui fu conca il cielo, sets the final part (lines 73–85) of a pastoral dialogue written by Guarini in 1579 to celebrate the wedding between Margherita and Duke Alfonso II d’Este, Dimmi, gentil pastore (but often printed under the name of Tasso in contemporary sources).19 All this is well-known, at least since Chater’s seminal article of 1978:20 what seems to have gone unnoticed so far is that the final piece of our book, Cantiam la bella Clori, is nothing but an adaptation from the immediately previous section of the same dialogue by Guarini (lines 60–72). Once again, the name Clori is purposely inserted a posteriori in the text (here, instead of “Dea” and “Reina”).21

As a result of this discovery, we have one more text by (in fact, adapted from) Guarini in Book VI a 5, and more doubts about the identity of Clori and the meaning of the whole Tirsi & Clori operation. Is this an old or a new piece? Did Marenzio set the excerpt from Guarini’s dialogue back in 1579, for Margherita’s wedding, and publish it, in a revised version, fifteen years later? Were the textual changes meant to severe the link with the 1579 event, and with Margherita, in order to make possible a reuse of the piece on a different occasion and for a different purpose? Then, why to reaffirm the original connection the following year, dedicating (or re-dedicating) the ‘sequel’ piece Lucida perla a cui fu conca il cielo to the initial dedicatee? Delicate issues of compositional chronology are at stake,22 and further research is needed to answer, or at least to reframe, these questions.

Returning to Tirsi and Clori, and to their role within the structure of Book VI a 5, Mauro Calcagno has recently proposed to read the entire book as a coherent narrative, with three alternating ‘voices’ (Tirsi, Clori, and the Narrator) and four sections: the “separation between the lovers” (nos. 1–9), is followed by a phase of “rapprochement and intimacy” (nos. 10–13), then by “death” (nos. 14–15), and by the “final attainment of love” (nos. 16–17).23 As further pointed out by Calcagno, this “script” seems to interact with other ordering criteria: the madrigals of the first “section”, for instance, are all written in low clefs (c1c3c4c4f4), but other, essentially musical, structuring principles are clearly at work (see Table 2).

All the scholars who have studied this book, at least since Alfred Einstein, have underscored the stylistic shift from Marenzio’s previous collections.24 In line with the innovative poetic choices discussed above, Marenzio developed a new lyric-declamatory style that went beyond the somehow fragmented aesthetic of madrigalism and allowed him to convey in a different, more dramatic way the intense expressivity of the texts. In all the pieces but no. 8 a single soprano is opposed to four lower voices (generally ATTB), often to achieve a more direct association with the ‘acting’ persona it musically represents.25 As penetratingly remarked by Bizzarini, however, the book does not have a “unified character”, but is rather inspired by a “subtly pluralistic aesthetic”:26 the touching monologues and laments on texts by Guarini and Tasso (which Einstein called “actual scenes”)27 alternate with lighter pieces (no. 10, with multi-voice ornamental flourishes and a dialogic structure; no. 16, with dance-like triple time sections; and the celebratory no. 17 for two choirs);28 other compositions experiment with daring chromaticisms and harmonic progressions (e.g., no. 5), echo certain features of contemporary Roman madrigals (no. 13), or even display an arioso character (no. 9).

Compared to some of the most famous books by Marenzio, such as the first and the last in the series a 5, Book VI has apparently had a modest success:29 republished in its entirety (with the exception of no. 17) in the comprehensive collections of Marenzio’s five-voice madrigals issued by Kauffmann in Nuremberg (1601) and by the Phalèse firm in Antwerp (1609 and 1632),30 it has had otherwise no reprints in anthologies. Madrigal no. 13, Mentre qual viva pietra, formed the basis for one of Girolamo Cavaglieri’s contrafacta31 and, according to Chater,32 worked as a model for a Magnificat a 6 by Michael Praetorius.33 No. 3, Donna de l’alma mia, de la mia vita, was intabulated for the lute by Joachim van den Hove in his Delitiae mvsicae.34 A handful of pieces were copied in English manuscripts35 (but a comprehensive mapping of Marenzio’s presence in manuscript sources is still to be done).

On the other hand, Severo Bonini, in his Discorsi e regole (c. 1653), singles out this book as an example of Marenzio’s proficiency in what he calls “terzo ordine” and de facto corresponds to Monteverdi’s seconda pratica.36 Furthermore, a systematic investigation about the texts set by Marenzio in Book VI a 5 and their concordances in other music collections indirectly attests to a favorable reception of the book: Marenzio was often the first to choose texts which later became popular among madrigalists (a clear example is no. 5, Udite lagrimosi).

* * *

The princeps of the book, issued by Angelo Gardano in Venice in 1594,37 is a remarkably accurate and correct edition, and the extant re-editions of 1603 and 1614 are also substantially reliable. The 1614 re-edition may derive directly from the princeps, based on the variants and on details of the mise-en-page and of ‘calibration’.38 Pending a systematic recognition of the extant copies of the princeps with Aruspix,39 I have been able to detect only two cases of stop-press correction. One occurs on the dedication page of the Basso partbook: while the copy held at Modena, Biblioteca Estense Universitaria (I-MOe), has a misprint in the signature: “Luca Marenzo” (!), all the other consulted copies have the correct reading. The other case regards page 8 in the Tenore partbook (madrigal no. 8, Ben ho del caro oggetto i sensi privi): in the copies held in Berkeley, Brussels, and London, the d3 at m. 16 is a semiminima instead of a minima; the other consulted copies have the correct reading.
