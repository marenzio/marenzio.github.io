Christine Jeanneret

1. Marenzio and the Madrigal in Rome

When Marenzio published his Primo libro di madrigali a quattro voci in 1585, he was at the service of Luigi d’Este since 1578, an appointment that will last one more year, until the cardinal’s death in December 1586. His connection with D’Este has given a fundamental impetus to Marenzio’s career.1 1585 is also an eventful year for the papacy in Rome: Pope Gregory XIII died on April 10th, 1585, and Felice Peretti is elected as Sixtus V thirteen days later with the support of Luigi d’Este. During the last year of Gregory’s pontificate, Marenzio published six books of music.2 His output dropped drastically after the election of the new pope. The only volume he published is the Primo libro dei Madrigali a Quattro Voci with a dedication to Marcantonio Serlupi, signed in Rome on July 15th, 1585. Not much is know about the dedicatee himself, a low-ranking nobleman in the papal court. He was a prelate of the Apostolic Chamber, member of a famous Roman family of ancient lineage, who held hereditary roles (called diritto di baldacchino).3 As Marenzio indicates in his dedication, he was certainly a music lover: “che per esser egli intendissimo di questa scientia” (because you are a great connoissor of this science [the science of music]). Serlupi is also the dedicatee of a madrigal book by Felice Anerio.

The re-editions of Marenzio’s book are either published with the same dedication, always signed in 1585, or without dedication at all. An extremely interesting exception is Vincenzi’s re-edition in 1587. This is most certainly a pirated edition, as attested by the new dedication to Michiele Boot, and not Booth as stated on the title page.4 The dedication is signed by Vincenzi and not by Marenzio. Boot was born 1566 in Breda and was a Flemish merchant in silk and cereals, as well as a magistrate.5 He seems to have had interests both in Italy for his business, and in music, since he is known to have had contacts with a singer in Venice,6 and –if Vincenzi’s text is reliable–also in Naples. Vincenzi had to find a new dedicatee in order to hide the fact that he re-edited the same book without privilege, and Boot provided the funds.

The four-voice texture is uncommon at the end of the sixteenth century. It suggests a look back to the early madrigal, as well as the ideal of sobriety and gravitas.7 Until this publication, Marenzio always wrote for five voices or more,8 and he will never publish another madrigal book for this texture. The choices of the texts also point to an earlier practice. Petrach predominates with eight settings, Sannazaro with five, only one by Tasso, and no Guarini at all. In this book Marenzio uses another archaic feature, the tempus diminutum, much more present here than in any other books. The volume opens with Petrarch’s Non vidi mai dopo notturna pioggia to emphasize the high literary choices and closes with a three-part eglogue by Sannazaro, Vienne Montan, mentre le nostre tormora, an example of the pastoral style. The volume therefore is two-fold, both in the choices of poetic texts and the musical setting, oscillating between the tragic and the arcadian tones, the archaic and the modern repertory.

Marenzio’s musical settings is very subtle and works on several levels. Some cases exemplify their sophistication. The madrigal Dissi a l’amata mia lucida stella presents an interesting dialogue. Marenzio creates various levels of complex repetitions. The melodic motive of ascending eighth-notes is always associated with the death of the first narrator (morirò), whereas the motive of decending quarter-notes is always associated with the mention of first narrator’s death in the second narrator’s words (morirai).

In Petrarch’s O bella man, an exquisite madrigalism –for the eyes only– is used on the words cinque perle (five pearls) represented musically by five white semibreves.

2. Printers and Printing techniques

The Primo libro a Quattro has the peculiarity of being printed in Rome, by Alessandro Gardano, and not in Venice like the vast majority of Marenzio’s works. Until 1584, he published everything with Angelo Gardano in Venice. Starting with the Quarto Libro dei Madrigali a Cinque Voci (1584), he uses the services of other printers as well, Scotto and heirs, as well as the newcomers on the Venetian printing market, Vincenzi and Amadino. Many of his re-editions were published by Angelo Gardano from 1584 onwards, but we have to wait until 1591 to find another editio princeps with him, the Quinto Libro di Madrigali a Sei Voci. From then on, he will again publish all his first editions with Gardano in Venice, until his death.

Since the dissolution of the Dorico house in 1572, there was a lacuna in Roman printing. Alessandro Gardano, the eldest son of Antonio moved to Rome between 1581 and 1583, leaving the Venetian press to his brother Angelo.9 Alessandro started a partnership with Francesco Coattino, lasting at least six years, until 1589. He then leaves his share to Coattino, who continues music printing until 1594. All the first editions by Palestrina during this time were made by Gardano-Coattino. In Rome, Gardano musical output includes sacred works by Anerio, Guerrero, Palestrina and Victoria. Marenzio’s Primo Libro a Quattro is therefore published at the very beginning of the Gardano’s activity in Rome. The princeps editio is realized with care and do not present many mistakes. The collation with the various re-editions is nonetheless interesting, since it provides an insight in the transmission and filiation of the different volumes.

3. Filiation of the Primo libro a Quattro Voci

The Primo Libro a Quattro Voci is re-edited eight times between 1587 and 1608, mostly by Venetian printers, but also abroad by Le Roy et Ballard in Paris in 1598, then by Kauffman in Nurnberg (1603), and eventually by Phalèse in Antwerp in 1607. Many pieces will also appear in anthologies publish mostly in Northern Europe, with the interesting translations of seven pieces by Thomas Watson in his First Sett of Italian Madrigalls English, as well as numerous arrangements for lute and keyboard.10

Amadino’s re-edition of 1587 follows closely the layout of the princeps editio, but uses some more space, especially for the two-part pieces, where the first part often ends on the first line of the second part. This is a standard choice; Amadino used the original edition as a model and knew beforehand how much space he would need for each piece. The first piece, though, starts immediately under the dedication for the Canto, Alto and Tenor parts, with the first two staves on this page.

Vincenzi pirated the edition the same year with the dedication to Boot. His layout almost exactly follows Amadino’s edition. This is not a surprise, since both printers were partners from 1583 to 1586 and even after they split up, they continued to share their typographical material and privileges. This edition, however, seems to have been made with great rush and little care. It is full of errors in the music, the text-setting, and the layout. There are several errors in page numbering, especially in the bass partbook.

Angleo Gardano published a re-edition in 1592 with a different layout. He uses other ornate letters, sets the page differently, and adopts another policy for abbreviation or accentuation in the text-setting than his brother Alessandro. He seems to have used Amadino’s or Vincenzi’s editions as sources, since they share common errors and do not appear in the princeps. As is can be easily evidenced by the copying of mistakes, this re-edition will be the basis of all the successive re-editions and not the princeps editio.11 It is striking to note that all the mistakes introduced for the first time by Gardano in 1592 are faithfully copied in all the successive re-editions. This edition was therefore probably the most circulated one among all the various editions and re-editions.

The first foreign edition appears in Paris in 1598 by Le Roy and Ballard. There are several mistakes in the texts, in particular gallicisms such as ‘Toutto’ instead of ‘Tutto’, erroneous use of double consonants, often misplaced: ‘Appolo’ instead of ‘Apollo’, or misreadings ‘viatù’ instead of ‘virtù’, all indicating that the printer was a French speaker who had not a good knowledge of Italian.

The second re-edition by Angelo Gardano is published in 1603, eleven years after his first re–edition. He changes the vertical format to an oblong format. The latter was the traditional format for printing madrigals until 1580s, when the vertical format became fashionable. The motivations for changing formats were mostly commercial. Gardano using again an archaic format might have been a marketing strategy. Reprinting a book almost twenty years after his first edition, for the second time in his press, he might have tried to bring some changes to the visual aspects of the book.

Kauffman re-edition is published the same year and is based on Gardano’s 1592 edition. He creates more space in his layout and contrarily to Le Roy and Ballard, has a Italian speaking typographer, who does not make any mistake in the text setting.

Phalèse uses the oblong format, as he always does, but he too uses Gardano’s 1592 as a model. The oltremontani seem to have had some trouble with the mensuration signs. Le Roy and Ballard often mixes the use of C and cut-C, whereas Phalèse never uses cut-C but always substitutes it with C. Phalèse also often inserts an erroneous prolatio in the regular C in duple meter. He is lacking fonts for the triple time mensurations and realizes them by combining two different symbols together.

The last re-edition by Scotto in 1608 returns to the vertical format. Also based on Gardano’s 1592 edition, it has been made with great care, correcting almost all the mistakes in the successive re-editions.
