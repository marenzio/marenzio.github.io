Di nettare amoroso ebro la mente / Sonar le labra, e vi restaro i segni

Torquato Tasso

Sonnet: ABBA ABBA CDE CDE

(Prima parte)
Di nettare amoroso ebro la menteDrunk on Love’s nectar
Ratto fui, né so come, in chiusa chiostra,I was taken, not knowing how, into a clostered court,
E due belle d’amor guerrier’in giostraand two fair Amazons in jousting
Vidi con l’arme ond’egli è sì possente. I saw with the weapons that make Love so powerful.
5Vidi ch’in dolce arringo alteramenteI saw them dispaying their beauty,
Fer pria di lor beltà leggiadra mostra, one by one, in a sweet arena,
Poi, movendosi incontra ove s’inostra then, moving towards their lips’ red
La bocca, si ferir di bacio ardente.they wounded each other with an ardent kiss.
(Seconda parte)
Sonar le labra, e vi restaro i segni Their lips sounded, and the scars remained
10Di colpi impressi. Amor, deh, perché a vòto of the strokes given. Oh Love, why deploy such arms
Tant’arme e tai percosse usar da scherzo?and such combat in jest?
Provinsi in vera pugna, e non si sdegni Let a true battle be fought, and let’s not avoid
Scontro d’amante. Amor, me tuo devotoa lover’s conflict. Oh Love, me as your true servant
Opponi all’una, o fra le due fa terzo. oppose to one, or let me be the third between them.

The translations are partially indebited to the ones by Barbara Reynolds in Luca Marenzio, The Complete Six Voice Madrigals. Vol IV: The Fourth Book of Madrigal for Six Voices, ed. John Steele and Suzanne Court (New York: Gaudia, 2002).

Notes

The Marenzio and the Tasso 1581 versions correspond; thus the composer could have derived his text from the print. In Vi, 1587, copy of I-Vnm only, “T. Tasso” is added by hand on the upper margin. For the use of the Aldine edition by Marenzio, see also the notes to n. 12.
